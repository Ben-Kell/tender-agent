TITLE:	Australian Industry CAPABILITY PLAN

DESCRIPTION and intended use

The Australian Industry Capability (AIC) Plan sets out the Supplier’s strategy, plans, processes and responsibilities for achieving the AIC Objectives and satisfying the AIC Obligations, including describing the management of the AIC program for the Work Order Term.  To the maximum practicable extent, the AIC Plan is written to contain specific and measureable commitments by the Supplier.

The Supplier uses the AIC Plan to:

set out the strategies, plans, processes, responsibilities and timeframes for the delivery of the AIC program and achieving the AIC Objectives and satisfying the AIC Obligations;

manage, coordinate and monitor the delivery of the AIC program;

ensure that those parties (including AIC Subcontractors) who are undertaking AIC-related activities understand their responsibilities, the commitments to be achieved, and the time-frames involved;

integrate the AIC activities being performed by AIC Subcontractors with the Supplier’s AIC activities to ensure that a coherent and cohesive AIC program is realised; and

provide assurance to the Commonwealth that the Supplier’s plan for delivering the AIC program will enable the AIC Objectives and AIC Obligations to be achieved while satisfying the other requirements of the Work Order.

The Commonwealth uses the AIC Plan to:

evaluate and gain assurance that the Supplier’s AIC program will achieve the AIC requirements of the Work Order;

provide a basis for monitoring and assessing the Supplier’s performance in relation to the execution of the AIC program;

assess the Supplier’s willingness and ability to work collaboratively with the Commonwealth and Australian Industry to achieve the AIC Objectives and AIC Obligations; and

identify the Commonwealth’s involvement in the AIC program.

PREPARATION INSTRUCTIONS

Generic Format and Content

The AIC Plan shall comply with the general format, content and preparation instructions contained in this document.

When the Work Order has specified delivery of another plan that contains aspects of the required information, the AIC Plan shall summarise these aspects and refer to the other plan.

The AIC Plan shall be written in accordance with the following requirements:

the Supplier’s commitments are to be separated from any background or descriptive information;

the background or descriptive information is to be no more than 25% of the overall size of the AIC Plan (measured by page count), and is to be clearly identified as background and/or descriptive material;

the Supplier’s commitments are to be set out using the following terminology as appropriate:

except when using one of the forms in paragraph (ii) below, the words, “the Supplier shall […]”, are to be used to set out absolute commitments;

the words, “the Supplier shall use its best endeavours to […]” or “the Supplier shall use its reasonable endeavours to […]”, are to be used when the Supplier is not able to make an absolute commitment, but a qualified commitment is required; and

the words, “the Supplier may […]”, are to be used when there is little-to-no certainty that a particular path or decision will actually occur or be made, or the matter is discretionary; and

any matters or assumptions which qualify the Supplier’s commitments are to be clearly stated individually in respect of each commitment, and the relevant commitment is to be expressed to be subject to those qualifications using specific cross references.  Any background or descriptive information is not to qualify the Supplier’s commitments.  Generic words, such as “where appropriate”, “when appropriate”, or “as required” are not to be used to qualify the Supplier’s commitments.

The AIC Plan is subject to Approval in accordance with the requirements of the Work Order and Statement of Work.  To allow for more objective assessment of the AIC Plan, Approval will be based upon the following criteria:

the program of AIC activities achieves the AIC Objectives and satisfies the AIC Obligations;

the overall Australian Contract Expenditure (ACE) for the Work Order and the Prescribed ACE Percentages are reasonable and achievable in the context of the Services being acquired;

any update to the AIC Plan is traceable to the preceding version and does not:

derogate from the commitments in the preceding version (unless otherwise agreed, in advance and in writing, by the Commonwealth Representative);

increase the risk of not satisfying the AIC Obligations; or

affect the Prescribed ACE Percentages unless supported by a Work Order change, as applicable.

Joint Capabilities Group (JCG) is a multi-domain Defence Group that delivers military effects across the integrated force. JCG consists of commands, divisions, and organic elements which include Headquarters JCG (HQJCG), Joint Capabilities Division, Cyber Command, Joint Logistics Command, Space Command, Cyber Operations Division and Military Strategic Effects. The objective of JCG is to train and prepare space, cyber, information and logistics forces, maintain standing components for space, cyber and logistics for Chief Joint Operations (CJOPS) and ensure capabilities deliver critical warfighting edge across the five domains. The Directorate of Digital Identity Services (DDIS), within Enterprise Technology Operations Branch (ETOB) is required to operate and maintain eTools and DDM, which are critical elements of Defence’s wider Identity & Access Management capability. eTools is a bespoke application suite developed by Defence consisting of: My Account Management Online (MAMO), a user facing, self-service, automated application for on boarding, off boarding and management of standard network accounts, contractor identities and group mailboxes, as well as reporting and administrator functions. Defence Account Retirement Service (DARS), an automated service that identifies, disabled and deletes standard network accounts that do not comply with ICT security policy. Defence Enterprise Administrator (DEA), an administrator facing application providing a front end interface for administration of Active Directory, Exchange, Skype and Filer operations. The DDM services suite utilise the Identity Bridge COTS product and SQL products with extensive Defence scripting to provide the following services: Defence Identity Service: the central point for the registration, management and historical auditing of unique Defence Digital Identities. This is the authoritative source used by Certificate Services and access management services used across Defence networks. Defence Corporate Directory (DCD): The Directory provides a single point of aggregated authoritative Defence information. Using the DCDWEB, a web dotnet frontend, allows  Defence Users ability to search. Defence application can also access the DCD information via approved connections. The Directory also provides information that is used by Military Messaging. Pegasus Directory Service (PDS): part of the DCD Service, which supports external Partners. This service is primarily executed in the Secret environment but expanding to Protected. The PDS service allows subsets of data to be shared with the 5eyes Partner Nations (CAN, UK, NZL, and USA). Data Exchange Service: provides the Department of Defence with the business capability to manage the migration, integration, and matching of data across multiple environments and systems. Using data synchronisation tools, Identity Bridge (IB) and SQL Server Integration Services (SSIS), the required data is: Extracted from the source; Manipulated; and Inserted into a destination for client use. Address Book Sync (ABSYNC) Service: ABSYNC service supports Defence-Wide mail through the synchronisation of mail objects and directory data between internal and external mail systems, as well as publishes mail system entries into the Defence Corporate Directory. User Info Provisioning (UIP) Service provides the strategic Active Directories (DPE/DSE) update information for their users. AD List/Group Management (ADDL) Service provides strategic Active Directories (DPE/DSE) by managing Distribution Groups/Group membership based on attributes not held by the active Directories. eTools and DDM exist in both the Protected and Secret domains, interface with a number of Defence systems to provide enabling services/data. Directly or indirectly, eTools and DDM have thousands of users and requests per day and are critical enablers of Defence business and security.
